# Frequently Asked Questions — Customs Call Centre

**Source:** HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) — Customs Call Centre FAQ Reference  
**Covers:** Most common queries received by the customs call centre  
**Last reviewed:** January 2024

---

## Section 1: Shipment Status

### Q1.1: How do I check the status of my shipment?
You can check your shipment status by providing one of the following identifiers:
- **Tracking ID** (e.g., TRK-2024-08812) — assigned by your freight forwarder
- **Declaration ID / Reference number** (e.g., INV-2024-00456) — shown on your customs declaration acceptance notification
- **MRN (Movement Reference Number)** — the unique reference on your customs declaration

Our agent can look up any of these against the customs records. Have your identifier ready before calling.

---

### Q1.2: What does the status code mean?

| Status | Plain-Language Meaning |
|---|---|
| **PENDING** | Declaration received but not yet processed |
| **IN_TRANSIT** | Goods are en route to the UK — not yet arrived |
| **UNDER_EXAMINATION** | Selected for physical or document examination |
| **HELD** | Goods detained pending investigation or compliance check |
| **CLEARED** | Goods released — customs process complete |
| **SEIZED** | Goods confiscated by customs |
| **UNDER_APPEAL** | Importer has appealed a duty or classification decision |

---

### Q1.3: My shipment shows HELD. What does that mean and what should I do?
A hold means customs has detained the goods while an issue is investigated. Common reasons for holds:

1. **Document check hold** — A required document is missing or unclear. You or your broker should contact the port of entry directly to provide the missing document. This is typically resolved within 24–48 hours.

2. **Physical examination hold** — Goods have been selected for physical inspection. No action needed from you until the examination is complete. The examining officer will contact your broker if additional information is needed.

3. **Compliance hold** — A potential customs offence is being investigated (misdeclaration, valuation issue, prohibited goods). You will receive a formal Notice of Detention. You may want to seek legal advice before responding.

4. **Licensing hold** — An import licence or permit is required but was not presented. Obtain the required licence and submit it to the border force officer handling the case.

If your shipment has been held and you have not received a Notice of Detention within 3 working days, contact the port of entry's Customs Control team directly.

---

### Q1.4: How long does a physical examination take?
Physical examinations typically take 1–3 working days from when the examination slot is allocated. During peak periods (e.g., Christmas, post-summer) queues may extend this to 5 working days. If your goods are perishable or time-sensitive, contact the examination centre directly to request priority handling.

---

### Q1.5: My goods were cleared but the expected delivery hasn't arrived. Who should I contact?
Once customs releases goods, they are handed to your freight forwarder or customs broker for onward transport. Customs does not track goods after release. Contact your freight forwarder or shipping agent with the release notification reference to track your delivery.

---

### Q1.6: Can I get a customs seal number for my container?
Contact the port's Temporary Storage or examination centre. Customs seal numbers are issued by the examining officer and are recorded against the declaration. Your broker will also have this information.

## Section 2: Duty and Tax Queries

### Q2.1: How is my customs duty calculated?
Customs duty = Customs Value (CIF) × Duty Rate.

**Customs Value** is typically the invoice price plus freight and insurance costs to the UK port of entry (CIF basis).

**Duty Rate** depends on your commodity (HS) code and the country of origin. See the Duty Calculation Guide for worked examples.

---

### Q2.2: I think my duty amount is wrong. What can I do?
You can submit a **Post-Clearance Amendment (C285 form)** within 3 years of the date the declaration was accepted. This can be used to:
- Claim a repayment if you overpaid
- Correct an error in the original declaration
- Claim a late preference if you forgot to present origin evidence

You will need to provide supporting evidence (correct HS code justification, corrected invoice, certificate of origin, etc.).

---

### Q2.3: Why am I being charged 12% duty when my goods come from Bangladesh?
Bangladesh qualifies for 0% preferential duty under the UK-DCTS scheme for most textile goods — but only if you provide valid origin evidence at the time of declaration. You need either:
- A **Form A certificate of origin** issued by the Bangladesh Export Promotion Bureau (EPB), or
- A **REX (Registered Exporter) statement** printed or stamped on the commercial invoice

If neither was presented with your declaration, HMRC applies the standard MFN rate of 12%. You can claim a refund retrospectively within 3 years by obtaining the origin evidence from your supplier and submitting a C285 form.

---

### Q2.4: What is a deferred duty account (DDA)?
A Duty Deferment Account (DDA) allows approved importers to defer all customs duty and import VAT payments to a single monthly payment at the end of the calendar month, rather than paying at the time of each import. 

To apply:
1. You (or your bank) must provide a bank guarantee or a financial standing approval from HMRC
2. Apply using form C1200 (or your broker can set up a DDA in their name and defer on your behalf)
3. Once approved, duty is due on the 15th of the following month (or next working day)

---

### Q2.5: I received a duty demand after my goods were released. Is this normal?
Yes — HMRC has up to 3 years to issue a **Post-Clearance Demand Note (C18)** if they identify an underpayment after clearance. This typically happens following an audit, a physical examination result that revealed misdeclaration, or an intelligence referral. 

The C18 demand will specify the amount owed, the reason, and the deadline for payment. You have 30 days to pay or to submit a formal objection. Interest is charged from the date of the original declaration.

---

### Q2.6: What is import VAT, and is it different from customs duty?
Yes, they are separate charges:
- **Customs Duty** — charged on the customs value (CIF). Varies by commodity and origin.
- **Import VAT** — charged at 20% on (customs value + customs duty). Most VAT-registered businesses reclaim this as input tax.

The **Postponed VAT Accounting (PVA)** scheme lets VAT-registered importers account for import VAT on their VAT return rather than paying at the border. This significantly improves cash flow. Non-VAT-registered importers must pay import VAT at the border.

## Section 3: Documentation

### Q3.1: What documents does customs need to clear my shipment?

**Mandatory for all imports:**
- Commercial invoice (showing price, quantity, description, buyer/seller details)
- Packing list
- Bill of Lading (sea) or Air Waybill (air)
- Customs declaration (submitted via CDS by your broker)

**Conditionally required:**
- Certificate of origin / REX statement (if claiming preferential rate)
- Import licence (for restricted goods)
- Phytosanitary certificate (for plant products)
- Health certificate (for animal products)
- CITES permit (for endangered species)
- CE/UKCA marking documentation (for regulated products)

---

### Q3.2: My Form A certificate of origin was rejected. Why?
Common reasons for Form A rejection:
1. **Serial number not verified** — HMRC cannot confirm the Form A serial number with the issuing authority (EPB Bangladesh, EPB Cambodia, etc.)
2. **Description mismatch** — the goods description on the Form A does not match the invoice
3. **Value inconsistency** — the declared value on the customs declaration is higher than the value on the Form A
4. **Late issuance** — the Form A was issued after the goods were shipped (only permitted in exceptional circumstances)
5. **Signature missing** — the authorised signatory signature or stamp is absent

If your Form A was rejected, contact your supplier to obtain a replacement or corrected certificate. You can then submit a C285 form to claim the preferential rate with the new certificate.

---

### Q3.3: What is a REX statement and how is it different from Form A?
The **Registered Exporter (REX)** system is a self-certification system. A REX statement is a declaration made by a registered exporter on the commercial invoice (or delivery note), rather than requiring a separate government-issued certificate.

The REX number of the exporter must appear on the statement. To verify a REX number, check the REX database on the EU or UK customs authority website.

REX statements are accepted as equivalent to Form A for UK-DCTS purposes.

---

### Q3.4: My bill of lading shows a different consignee from the customs declaration. Is this a problem?
Yes — the consignee on the bill of lading, commercial invoice, and customs declaration must match. Mismatches can trigger a document check hold. If there is a legitimate reason for the difference (e.g., a back-to-back transaction or buyer change), provide a letter of explanation with the declaration.

---

## Section 4: HS Code and Classification

### Q4.1: How do I find the correct HS code for my product?
Use the **UK Trade Tariff** tool at [trade.gov.uk](https://www.trade.gov.uk). Enter a description of your product and the tool suggests relevant HS codes and shows the applicable duty rates and any licensing requirements.

For complex products or borderline classifications, HMRC offers a free **Advance Tariff Ruling (ATR)** service. An ATR is legally binding for 3 years, providing certainty over the classification and duty rate. Apply via the HMRC online portal.

---

### Q4.2: My goods were reclassified by customs. What can I do?
If customs has reclassified your goods to a higher-duty code, you have two options:
1. **Pay the revised assessment** — customs will issue a C18 demand note. You have 30 days to pay. Interest applies from the original declaration date.
2. **Appeal the reclassification** — submit a formal appeal within 30 days of the reclassification decision. Include your own classification evidence (ATR ruling, trade publication evidence, technical specifications, similar goods precedents from UK or EU ruling databases).

If you have an existing ATR ruling that supports your original classification, present this immediately — it will take precedence over the officer's reclassification.

---

### Q4.3: My goods are garments. How do I know whether they are classified as industrial workwear or fashion clothing?
This is one of the most common classification disputes in the textile sector. The key distinction:

- **Industrial/protective workwear (HS 6113, 6114):** Must be specifically designed and marketed for professional protection (e.g., oil-resistant, cut-resistant, flame-retardant). They typically carry protective clothing standards (EN ISO 20471, EN 13034, EN ISO 11612, etc.) and are not suitable for general fashion wear.

- **Fashion garments (HS 6101–6117):** General-purpose garments including denim jeans, cotton trousers, T-shirts, etc. Standard retail garments are almost always classified in this section regardless of the declared intended use.

A garment cannot be classified as industrial protective clothing unless it carries the appropriate certification. If a physical examination reveals fashion garments declared as industrial workwear, this is treated as HS code misclassification fraud.

## Section 5: Escalation and Appeals

### Q5.1: I believe I'm being treated unfairly. How do I complain?
You can complain about the conduct of a customs officer or the handling of your case through HMRC's formal complaints procedure:
1. First, contact the supervising officer at the relevant port
2. If unresolved, submit a formal complaint to HMRC Customer Service
3. If still unresolved, escalate to the Independent Adjudicator for HMRC (free of charge)

---

### Q5.2: My goods were seized. Can I get them back?
If goods are seized, you will receive a Notice of Seizure. You have **one calendar month** from the date of seizure to submit a Notice of Claim (in writing to the Border Force Legal team) if you believe the seizure was incorrect. 

If you do not respond within one month, the goods are forfeit. If you submit a claim, the case is heard before a Magistrates' Court (or in Scotland, a Sheriff Court). If the seizure is upheld, goods are forfeited; if overturned, goods are returned.

For seized goods with a customs duty shortfall, you may also be issued with a civil penalty. Criminal prosecution is possible for serious cases.

---

### Q5.3: How long can customs hold my goods before they must release or charge me?
Customs has the right to hold goods pending investigation without a fixed deadline. However, if goods are held for more than 21 days without formal detention or seizure, you can apply to the court for their release. If customs refuses to release goods, they must formally seize them and follow the seizure procedure (see Q5.2).

In practice, most routine holds (document checks, routine physical examinations) are resolved within 5 working days. Extended holds typically relate to criminal investigations or major valuation disputes.

---

## Section 6: Authorised Economic Operator (AEO)

### Q6.1: What is AEO status and how does it help?
**Authorised Economic Operator (AEO)** status is a certification granted by HMRC to trusted traders who have demonstrated high standards of compliance, financial solvency, and customs competence. AEO benefits include:
- Faster customs processing (Route 6 / Blue lane)
- Fewer physical examinations
- Priority treatment in document checks
- Mutual recognition with EU AEO programmes

There are two types:
- **AEOC (Customs Simplifications):** Allows simplified customs procedures
- **AEOS (Security and Safety):** Recognises the security standards of the business

---

### Q6.2: How do I apply for AEO?
Apply via the HMRC online portal. HMRC will conduct a formal audit of your customs records, financial standing, and security practices. The application process typically takes 60–120 days. You must have a compliant customs record for at least 3 years prior to applying.
