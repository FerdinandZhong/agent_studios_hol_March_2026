# UK Customs Import Process — Step-by-Step Guide

**Source:** HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) — Customs Call Centre Reference Document  
**Applies to:** Goods imported into Great Britain (England, Scotland, Wales) from outside the UK  
**Last reviewed:** January 2024

---

## Overview

Importing goods into the UK involves five key stages. Each stage has specific requirements, and delays or errors at any stage can result in additional costs, physical examinations, or goods being held.

```
STAGE 1          STAGE 2          STAGE 3          STAGE 4          STAGE 5
Pre-Arrival  →  Arrival &    →  Customs      →  Duty Payment  →  Release &
Planning        Presentation     Declaration      & Examination     Delivery
```

---

## Stage 1: Pre-Arrival Planning

Before your goods reach the UK, you need to prepare the following:

### 1.1 Commodity (HS) Code
Every product imported into the UK must be assigned the correct Harmonized System (HS) commodity code. The HS code determines:
- The rate of customs duty payable
- Whether any import licences or permits are needed
- Whether the goods qualify for a preferential tariff (reduced or zero duty rate)

You can look up the correct HS code using the UK Global Tariff tool at [trade.gov.uk](https://www.trade.gov.uk).

**Common mistake:** Declaring goods under a lower-duty HS code to reduce the duty payable. This is a customs offence and can result in penalties, interest charges, and goods seizure.

### 1.2 Customs Value
Customs duty is calculated on the **customs value** of the goods, not just the invoice price. The customs value is normally the **CIF (Cost + Insurance + Freight)** value — the price of the goods plus the cost of shipping and insurance to the UK port of entry.

### 1.3 Origin Documentation
If your goods are from a country with which the UK has a preferential trade agreement (e.g., Bangladesh, Cambodia, Sri Lanka under UK-DCTS), you need origin evidence to claim the reduced duty rate:
- **Form A** (GSP Certificate of Origin) — issued by the exporting country's designated authority
- **REX Statement** (Registered Exporter System) — printed on the commercial invoice by the exporter

Without valid origin documentation, the standard MFN (Most Favoured Nation) rate applies.

### 1.4 Import Licences and Permits
Certain goods require an import licence or permit before they can enter the UK:
- Firearms and weapons
- Controlled drugs and precursor chemicals
- Endangered species (CITES)
- Food and agricultural products with sanitary controls (DEFRA approval)
- Dual-use goods (items with both civilian and military applications — Export Control Joint Unit review required)
- Textiles from certain countries under safeguard measures

Check whether your goods require a licence before shipment. Unlicensed restricted goods will be seized at the border.

### 1.5 EORI Number
All importers must have an **Economic Operator Registration and Identification (EORI)** number. Without an EORI number, your goods cannot be cleared through UK customs. Apply at [gov.uk/eori](https://www.gov.uk/eori).

## Stage 2: Arrival and Presentation of Goods

### 2.1 Entry Summary Declaration (ENS)
For goods arriving by sea or air, the carrier or importer must submit an **Entry Summary Declaration** before the goods arrive. This pre-arrival notification allows customs to perform risk assessment before the vessel docks.

Timelines:
- **Sea freight (containerised):** At least 24 hours before loading at the foreign port
- **Sea freight (bulk/break bulk):** At least 4 hours before arrival
- **Air freight:** At least 4 hours before departure for short-haul, or before departure for long-haul

### 2.2 Temporary Storage
Upon arrival, goods are placed in **Temporary Storage** (TS) at the port. Goods must be presented to customs and a full customs declaration submitted within:
- **7 days** (sea and inland waterway)
- **3 days** (air and road)

If no declaration is submitted within this window, goods may be auctioned or destroyed.

### 2.3 Pre-Lodgement Declarations
Importers can submit their customs declaration before the goods arrive (pre-lodgement). This is recommended for routine shipments as it allows customs to complete risk assessment in advance, resulting in faster release.

## Stage 3: Customs Declaration

### 3.1 Submitting the Declaration
The customs declaration (an Import Declaration, formerly known as a C88) must be submitted via the UK's **Customs Declaration Service (CDS)**. Most importers use a licensed customs broker or freight forwarder to submit declarations on their behalf.

The declaration must include:
| Field | Description |
|---|---|
| Commodity code | Full 10-digit commodity code (HS + 4 additional digits) |
| Customs value | CIF value in GBP or original currency with exchange rate |
| Country of origin | ISO country code |
| Preference code | To claim preferential duty rate |
| Quantity | Weight in kg AND units where applicable |
| Incoterms | Trade terms (FOB, CIF, EXW, etc.) |
| Consignee EORI | Importer's EORI number |

### 3.2 Declaration Routes
Once submitted, the CDS system assigns the declaration to one of four routes:

| Route | Colour | Description | Action Required |
|---|---|---|---|
| Route 1 | Green | Immediate release — low risk | Goods released automatically |
| Route 2 | Yellow | Document check | Importer must submit supporting documents within 2 hours |
| Route 3 | Red | Physical examination | Goods moved to examination shed |
| Route 6 | Blue | AEO fast-track | Authorised Economic Operators — expedited processing |

### 3.3 Supporting Documents
For a document check (Route 2), you may need to provide:
- Commercial invoice
- Packing list
- Bill of lading or Air Waybill
- Certificate of origin (Form A or REX statement)
- Import licence (if applicable)
- Insurance certificate (CIF shipments)
- Any specific certificates (phytosanitary, conformity, etc.)

---

## Stage 4: Duty Payment and Physical Examination

### 4.1 Duty Payment
Once the declaration is accepted, customs calculates and demands the duty owed. Payment methods:
- **Immediate payment** via the CDS payment portal (credit/debit card or bank transfer)
- **Deferred duty account (DDA):** Duty payments are deferred to the end of the calendar month. Requires a bank guarantee and HMRC approval.
- **Duty deferment via a broker:** Your customs broker may hold a DDA and pay duty on your behalf, billing you later.

Goods will not be released until duty is paid (unless a DDA or other deferment arrangement is in place).

### 4.2 Physical Examination
Goods selected for physical examination (Route 3) are moved to an examination shed at the port. An examination may check:
- Does the quantity match the declaration?
- Do the goods match the declared description and HS code?
- Is the declared value plausible?
- Are prohibited or restricted items concealed?

A physical examination typically takes 1–3 working days. The importer or their broker must be available to facilitate access to the container.

### 4.3 Duty Assessment After Examination
If the examination reveals discrepancies (e.g., the goods do not match the HS code, or the quantity is higher than declared), customs will:
1. Issue a revised duty assessment
2. Demand payment of any additional duty plus interest
3. May issue a civil penalty for misdesclaration (typically 30% of the unpaid duty)

---

## Stage 5: Release and Delivery

### 5.1 Release to Importer
Once all checks are complete and duty is paid (or deferred), customs issues a **Release Notification**. The goods are released to the importer's freight forwarder for onward delivery.

### 5.2 Post-Clearance Amendment
If you believe a duty assessment is incorrect, you can submit a **Post-Clearance Demand (C285)** within 3 years of the date of acceptance. This allows you to claim a repayment if you overpaid, or correct an error in your original declaration.

### 5.3 Appeals
If customs issues a penalty or refuse to release goods, you have the right to appeal. You must submit a **Notice of Appeal (HMRC Form NDRC-1)** within 30 days of the decision. There is no charge for a first appeal to the Adjudicator.

## Key Timelines Summary

| Milestone | Deadline |
|---|---|
| Entry Summary Declaration (sea, containerised) | 24 hours before loading |
| Full declaration submission after arrival (sea) | Within 7 days of arrival |
| Document check (Route 2) | Within 2 hours of demand |
| Physical examination completion (typical) | 1–3 working days |
| Post-Clearance Amendment claim | Within 3 years of acceptance |
| Appeal against decision | Within 30 days of decision |

## Common Call Centre Queries and Quick Answers

**Q: Why are my goods held at the port?**  
A: Goods are typically held for one of three reasons: (1) document check — a document is missing or unclear; (2) physical examination — customs selected the shipment for inspection; (3) enforcement hold — a compliance issue requires investigation. We can look up the specific reason using your tracking ID.

**Q: How long will a physical examination take?**  
A: Physical examinations typically complete within 1–3 working days from the time the examination slot is allocated. We can check the current queue status for your port.

**Q: My goods have been cleared but the duty amount looks wrong.**  
A: You can submit a Post-Clearance Amendment (C285 form) within 3 years of the clearance date. We can provide guidance on the correct duty calculation using your HS code and customs value.

**Q: I'm importing for the first time. What do I need?**  
A: You'll need: (1) an EORI number — apply at gov.uk/eori; (2) the correct HS code for your goods; (3) a licensed customs broker to submit your declaration; (4) origin documentation if claiming a preferential rate.
